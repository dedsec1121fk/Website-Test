Ransomware 2025: Trends, Tactics and Countermeasures

Analysing the evolution of ransomware in 2025—from rising attacker groups and double extortion to declining payments—and outlining practical defenses and policy responses.

Cybersecurity 10 min read 2026-01-26

The State of Ransomware

Ransomware remains one of the most damaging cyber threats. In 2025 the threat landscape became more crowded and sophisticated. Rapid7’s annual report observed that at least 96 distinct ransomware groups were active—a 41% increase over the previous year【361019276269661†L37-L52】. More than 74% of campaigns now include data exfiltration or public leak threats in addition to encryption, forcing victims to negotiate under double or even triple extortion schemes【361019276269661†L94-L103】. Meanwhile, social engineering remains the dominant initial access vector, with over half of intrusions starting from compromised credentials or weak multi‑factor authentication【361019276269661†L60-L73】. Ransomware gangs are increasingly professional: they operate like businesses, rent their malware to affiliates, offer customer support and track payments.

Despite a proliferation of attackers, 2024 saw a 35% decline in total ransomware payments as companies, bolstered by law enforcement action and improved backups, increasingly refused to pay【903770772387732†L485-L519】. Verizon’s 2025 Data Breach Investigations Report found a 37% year‑over‑year increase in ransomware incidents, while the Dragos report noted an 87% rise in attacks on industrial environments, leading to partial or full shutdowns in 75% of cases【903770772387732†L521-L527】. In the UK, high‑profile campaigns against retail and automotive firms inflicted losses exceeding $400 million on Marks & Spencer and $2.5 billion on Jaguar Land Rover【903770772387732†L539-L580】, illustrating the scale of economic impact.

Anatomy of an Attack

Most ransomware incidents begin with social engineering: phishing emails, malicious attachments or SMS messages trick employees into divulging credentials. Attackers also exploit unpatched software vulnerabilities and misconfigured remote access services, especially in virtualised environments like VMware【361019276269661†L106-L134】. Once inside, they move laterally to gain domain administrator rights, disable backups and deploy malware across endpoints. Double extortion involves stealing sensitive data before encryption so that criminals can threaten to leak it if ransom is not paid. Some actors now target hypervisors to encrypt multiple virtual machines at once.

Payment negotiations often occur via anonymised chat platforms or TOR sites. Affiliates may offer victim support portals and proof‑of‑decryption services. Law enforcement operations disrupted major groups in recent years, but new actors rapidly fill the void. The decline in payments is encouraging, yet criminals adapt by diversifying income streams and targeting backup servers or cloud environments to maximise leverage.

Economic Impact and Response

Ransomware attacks impose costs far beyond ransom payments. Businesses lose revenue during downtime, incur remediation expenses and suffer reputational damage. Supply chains ripple as infected vendors halt operations. Government agencies and hospitals experience service disruptions that endanger lives. The 2025 attacks on UK retail and automotive companies illustrate that ransomware can cause billions in losses and shake investor confidence【903770772387732†L539-L580】. Even when organisations restore from backups, data breaches and leaks expose customers to identity theft and regulatory fines.

Governments respond with task forces, intelligence sharing and sanctions against threat actors. Law enforcement has disrupted several ransomware infrastructure networks and seized ransom payments. Insurance companies are tightening coverage requirements to encourage best practices. Yet, as payments decline, some criminals shift to pure data extortion or sabotage. The debate continues over whether paying ransoms fuels the criminal ecosystem; most experts advise against payment unless life is at stake.

Defense and Mitigation

Protecting against ransomware requires a multi‑layered approach. Risk‑based vulnerability management should prioritise patching of known flaws exploited by criminals【361019276269661†L106-L134】. Robust multi‑factor authentication and password managers prevent credential compromise. Continuous monitoring with endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools can spot suspicious behaviour early. Network segmentation and zero trust policies limit lateral movement. Regular, encrypted backups stored offline ensure recovery without paying ransom.

Education and awareness are equally important. Employees should be trained to recognise phishing attempts, report suspicious emails and follow cybersecurity policies. Organisations must develop incident response plans detailing who to contact, when to engage law enforcement and how to communicate with stakeholders. Collaboration with sector‑specific Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs) helps organisations stay informed about emerging threats. Ultimately, resilience comes from preparation, not payment.
