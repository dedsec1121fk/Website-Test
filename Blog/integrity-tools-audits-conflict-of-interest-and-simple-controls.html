Overview

Integrity Tools: Audits, Conflict-of-Interest, and Simple Controls is easier to act on when you treat it as a set of small, reversible choices. Instead of chasing “perfect” setups, aim for a workflow that still works on your worst day: low battery, rushed decisions, and noisy information. An organization reduces risk by separating duties and requiring conflict-of-interest declarations.

Key ideas

Three ideas to keep in mind:

  • Open data can turn rumors into checkable facts.
  • Talk about systems and controls—avoid naming individuals without evidence.
  • Corruption risk increases with opacity, discretion, and weak oversight.

Do this today

A practical way to apply this today:

  1. Start with public documents: decisions, tenders, budgets, audits.
  2. Look for patterns over time, not one-off stories.
  3. Use neutral language: describe what is observable and cite sources.

Pitfalls to avoid

Common pitfalls: (a) trusting a single screenshot or single setting, (b) adding tools you won’t maintain, and (c) skipping the ‘test’ step. If you can’t test it, you don’t really own it.

Reusable checklist

Quick checklist you can reuse:

  • Update software and firmware.
  • Turn on the strongest account protection available.
  • Reduce what you share (data, permissions, extensions).
  • Keep one backup path and one recovery path.
  • Write down the 3 steps you’ll do if something goes wrong.

Mini glossary

Mini glossary:

  • Conflict of interest: Private interest influencing public duty.
  • Procurement: Public purchasing process for goods/services.
  • Diavgeia: Greek transparency portal for public decisions.
  • Whistleblowing: Reporting wrongdoing through safe channels.

Wrap‑up

If you take only one thing from this post, make it this: choose fewer moving parts, document the few you keep, and rehearse once. That’s how “security” turns into muscle memory.
