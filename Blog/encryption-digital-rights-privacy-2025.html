Encryption and Digital Rights: Defending Privacy in 2025

Analyzes the importance of strong encryption, threats from proposed scanning laws, and the ongoing struggle between privacy and surveillance.

Law 10 min read 2026-01-26

The Essential Role of Encryption

In the digital age, encryption serves as the bedrock of privacy and security, protecting everything from personal messages to financial transactions and health records. End‑to‑end encryption ensures that only the intended recipients can read a communication; no intermediary — not even the service provider — can access the contents. This confidentiality fosters trust for journalists, human‑rights defenders, business executives and ordinary people. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) underscores that strong encryption is essential for private conversations and warns that undermining it would expose users to pervasive surveillance and abuse【368251624556076†L127-L141】. Encryption also provides integrity and authentication by preventing tampering and verifying that messages come from legitimate sources. Without it, the digital economy and civil liberties would be severely compromised. As more daily interactions move online and AI tools process sensitive data, the importance of robust encryption grows.

Government Proposals and Backdoors

Despite its critical role, governments around the world continue to propose measures that would weaken encryption under the guise of public safety. The European Union’s “Chat Control” proposal seeks to mandate client‑side scanning, requiring messaging platforms to scan all private communications on users’ devices before encryption【368251624556076†L131-L141】. Such scanning not only violates confidentiality but also creates vulnerabilities that attackers could exploit. In France, lawmakers floated the idea of allowing law‑enforcement “ghost participants” to silently join encrypted chats【368251624556076†L144-L148】; the proposal was ultimately rejected but highlighted the willingness of authorities to demand access to encrypted conversations. In the United Kingdom, the government reportedly pressured Apple to add a backdoor to iCloud, prompting the company to disable Advanced Data Protection for UK users【368251624556076†L152-L159】. Globally, proposals to require encryption backdoors or weaken protocols persist, often framed as necessary to combat terrorism or child exploitation. However, security experts warn that any deliberate vulnerability compromises all users.

Balancing Safety and Rights

Protecting children and combatting crime are legitimate goals, but policymakers must not create systems that erode fundamental rights. Laws such as the U.S. STOP CSAM Act would require providers to monitor content for child abuse material, effectively mandating surveillance of private communications【368251624556076†L127-L187】. The EFF argues that such measures threaten encryption, chilling free expression and enabling authoritarian regimes to justify similar monitoring【368251624556076†L127-L187】. Effective strategies to fight abuse include targeting perpetrators, investing in prevention and supporting victims, rather than scanning all messages. Privacy advocates caution that mass surveillance tools can be repurposed for political repression and discrimination. International human rights law recognises the right to privacy and freedom of expression; any limitation must be necessary, proportionate and subject to judicial oversight. Balancing safety and rights requires robust safeguards, transparency and independent oversight, along with strong security measures to protect data.

Advocacy and a Rights‑Based Future

Across the globe, civil society organisations, technologists and lawmakers are working to defend encryption and digital rights. Advocacy groups like the EFF call on governments to drop client‑side scanning proposals, to respect encryption and to consult security experts. Industry coalitions emphasise that secure communication is essential for trust in digital services. Policymakers in some jurisdictions recognise this: the French National Assembly rejected the “ghost participants” proposal【368251624556076†L144-L148】, and the European Parliament has sought to balance child‑protection measures with privacy safeguards. Protecting encryption also intersects with broader privacy and data protection initiatives, reinforcing the need for comprehensive laws that safeguard personal data while enabling responsible innovation. Moving forward, a rights‑based approach to technology development must centre on privacy, security, transparency and accountability. By resisting demands for backdoors and ensuring that surveillance powers are carefully limited, societies can uphold civil liberties and maintain the benefits of a secure digital ecosystem.
