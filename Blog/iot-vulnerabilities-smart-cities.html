IoT Vulnerabilities: When Smart Cities Become Attack Surfaces

Explore how smart city infrastructure—traffic lights, water treatment systems, and connected sensors—creates massive attack surfaces for hackers. Learn from real incidents like the Dallas siren hack and Oldsmar water treatment breach that demonstrate how Watch Dogs scenarios are becoming reality.

Tech & Culture 10 min read 2026-01-13

Introduction: Hacking a City

In Watch Dogs, Aiden Pearce can hack traffic lights to cause accidents, trigger steam pipe explosions, and manipulate infrastructure throughout Chicago. These abilities seemed fantastical in 2014—dramatic exaggerations designed to make compelling gameplay. A decade later, security researchers and real-world attackers have demonstrated that city infrastructure is far more vulnerable than anyone wanted to believe. The Internet of Things has transformed cities into vast, interconnected networks of sensors, controllers, and systems—and many of them were never designed with security in mind.

The stakes couldn't be higher. Traffic systems, water treatment plants, power grids, and emergency services all rely on networked systems that can potentially be accessed remotely. A successful attack on critical infrastructure could endanger lives, cause massive economic damage, and undermine public trust in the systems we depend on daily. This article examines real-world attacks on smart city infrastructure, explores why these systems are so vulnerable, and considers what the growing attack surface means for urban security.

The Dallas Siren Incident: Chaos at 11:42 PM

On the night of April 7, 2017, residents of Dallas, Texas were jolted awake by the blaring of emergency tornado sirens. All 156 of the city's outdoor warning sirens activated simultaneously and continued for over 90 minutes. Panicked residents flooded 911 with calls—the system received over 4,400 calls in that period. City officials initially couldn't identify the source of the problem, leading to speculation about equipment malfunction, software errors, or cyberattack.

Investigation revealed that someone had exploited vulnerabilities in the siren system's radio frequency controls. The sirens were activated using a radio signal, and the attacker had identified the frequency and protocol used to trigger them. No internet connection was required—just a radio transmitter and knowledge of the system's weaknesses. The city eventually had to physically disconnect power to the siren network to stop the noise. The attacker was never publicly identified.

The Dallas incident demonstrated how vulnerable city infrastructure could be. Emergency warning systems are designed to protect public safety, but their compromise could have the opposite effect—desensitizing residents to warnings or causing panic when none is warranted. The attack required no sophisticated hacking skills, just an understanding of radio protocols that security researchers had been warning about for years.

Oldsmar Water Treatment: A Near-Disaster

In February 2021, an attacker remotely accessed the water treatment plant in Oldsmar, Florida and attempted to poison the city's water supply. Using TeamViewer, a remote access application, the intruder changed the setting for sodium hydroxide (lye) from approximately 100 parts per million to 11,100 parts per million—a level that could cause severe burns and potentially death if consumed.

The attack was detected in real-time by an operator who noticed his cursor moving independently on the screen. He watched as the intruder navigated to the chemical controls and made the dangerous adjustment. The operator immediately reversed the change, and no contaminated water reached residents. However, the incident revealed alarming security practices: the plant was using outdated Windows 7 systems, all computers shared the same password for remote access, and the TeamViewer application was directly connected to the internet without a firewall.

The Oldsmar attack represents exactly the kind of infrastructure manipulation depicted in Watch Dogs. The ability to remotely control chemical levels in a water treatment plant—affecting potentially millions of people—was protected by nothing more than a shared password and consumer-grade remote access software. Subsequent investigations revealed that similar vulnerabilities exist in water utilities across the United States, with many small and mid-sized plants lacking the resources or expertise to implement proper security.

Traffic Systems: The Hackable Highway

In Watch Dogs, hacking traffic lights is one of the most common and useful abilities. Aiden Pearce can cause accidents, create traffic jams, and manipulate vehicle flow throughout the city. Security researchers have demonstrated that reality isn't far behind. Studies of traffic control systems in major American cities have revealed widespread vulnerabilities that could allow similar manipulation.

University of Michigan researchers demonstrated in 2014 that they could hack into traffic light systems controlling nearly 100 intersections. The systems used unencrypted wireless communications, default usernames and passwords, and had no authentication mechanisms. An attacker could change light timing, disable signals, or create dangerous conditions. The researchers reported their findings to authorities, but similar vulnerabilities persist in traffic systems worldwide.

Connected Vehicles and V2X

Modern vehicles increasingly communicate with infrastructure through Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) technology. Cars can receive traffic light timing information, road condition warnings, and emergency vehicle alerts. While these systems promise improved safety and efficiency, they also create new attack vectors. Researchers have demonstrated spoofing attacks that could send false traffic information to vehicles, potentially causing them to accelerate through red lights or brake unexpectedly.

The integration of autonomous vehicles adds another layer of concern. Self-driving cars rely on sensor data and infrastructure communications to navigate safely. If those systems can be manipulated, the consequences could be catastrophic. A coordinated attack on traffic infrastructure could potentially cause widespread accidents, traffic paralysis, or both.

Power Grids and Utility Systems

The vulnerability of power systems to cyberattack has been dramatically demonstrated multiple times. In December 2015, hackers attacked Ukraine's power grid, causing blackouts affecting approximately 230,000 people. The attack used malware to take control of utility systems, open circuit breakers, and disable the control systems that could have been used to restore power. It was the first confirmed cyberattack to take down a power grid.

A year later, Ukraine was hit again with a more sophisticated attack. The Industroyer malware was specifically designed to attack industrial control systems, demonstrating an escalation in the capabilities of infrastructure attackers. Security researchers have warned that similar attacks could target American and European power systems, potentially causing much larger and longer-lasting blackouts.

Smart Meters and the Connected Grid

The modernization of power grids has introduced millions of connected devices—smart meters, sensors, and automated controls. While these improve efficiency and enable faster response to outages, they also expand the attack surface dramatically. Each connected device is a potential entry point for attackers. Researchers have demonstrated vulnerabilities in smart meters that could be exploited to steal electricity, cause billing fraud, or potentially disrupt service.

Why Smart City Infrastructure Is So Vulnerable

Legacy Systems and Technical Debt

Much of our critical infrastructure was built decades ago, long before cybersecurity was a consideration. Water treatment plants, power stations, and traffic systems often run on industrial control systems designed for reliability and longevity, not security. These systems might use Windows XP, proprietary protocols with known vulnerabilities, or hardware that cannot be updated. Replacing them is enormously expensive and disruptive, so they continue operating with known security flaws.

The Connectivity Rush

The push to make cities "smart" has often prioritized functionality over security. Vendors sold connected devices and systems with promises of efficiency gains and cost savings, without adequately addressing the security implications. Many IoT devices used in city infrastructure ship with default passwords, lack encryption, have no update mechanisms, or contain fundamental security flaws. The rush to deploy connected systems outpaced the development of security standards and best practices.

Resource Constraints

Many cities and utilities operate with limited budgets and staff. Small water districts, rural power cooperatives, and underfunded municipal departments often lack dedicated cybersecurity personnel. They may not have the expertise to properly configure security systems, monitor for intrusions, or respond to incidents. The Oldsmar water plant that was nearly compromised served about 15,000 people—too small to justify a full security team, but still critical to public safety.

The Growing Attack Surface

As cities deploy more connected devices, the attack surface grows exponentially. Streetlights with internet connectivity, smart parking meters, environmental sensors, waste management systems, and countless other devices create entry points that attackers can exploit. Each device that connects to city networks is a potential vulnerability. The Shodan search engine, which indexes internet-connected devices, reveals thousands of industrial control systems, traffic cameras, and building management systems accessible from the public internet.

The interconnection between systems creates additional risks. A weakness in one system could provide access to others. An attacker who compromises a building's HVAC system might be able to pivot to more critical networks. The integration that makes smart cities efficient also makes them vulnerable—if everything is connected, everything is potentially at risk.

Conclusion: The Watch Dogs Warning

Watch Dogs presented infrastructure hacking as entertainment—a power fantasy where players could control cities from their phones. The reality, as demonstrated by incidents from Dallas to Oldsmar to Ukraine, is far less entertaining. Real attacks on infrastructure endanger lives, disrupt essential services, and expose the vulnerabilities in systems we take for granted. The game's depiction of hackable cities was meant to be a warning. That warning has proven prescient.

The challenge facing cities is immense. They must secure vast networks of devices and systems, many of which were never designed for the connected world. They must do so with limited budgets, against sophisticated adversaries, while continuing to provide essential services. The alternative—leaving critical infrastructure vulnerable to attack—is unacceptable. As cities become smarter, they must also become more secure, or the very systems designed to improve our lives could become weapons against us.
