Cybersecurity Regulations and Compliance in 2025: Navigating a Changing Legal Landscape

Explores new cybersecurity regulations like NIS2, DORA, the Cyber Resilience Act and CIRCIA, and offers guidance for organisations on compliance and resilience.

Law 10 min read 2026-01-26

Rising Threats and the Need for Regulation

Cybersecurity incidents have escalated in frequency, scale and impact, forcing governments around the world to enact stronger laws. Ransomware campaigns now regularly cripple hospitals, pipeline operators and local governments. Supply chain attacks against widely used software highlight systemic weaknesses. Rapid digitalisation has increased the attack surface, while remote work and IoT adoption have blurred the boundaries of corporate networks. In response, policymakers in 2025 are adopting comprehensive cybersecurity regulations that move beyond voluntary guidelines to impose mandatory controls, reporting obligations and accountability at the highest levels. The EU’s Digital Operational Resilience Act and NIS2 Directive embody this shift by requiring financial institutions and critical‑sector operators to integrate cybersecurity and operational resilience into their core processes【960195212561326†L916-L945】【960195212561326†L947-L968】. Meanwhile, other jurisdictions are strengthening incident reporting requirements and supply chain risk management. These measures recognise that safeguarding digital infrastructure is as essential as physical security and that resilience must be embedded in policy and practice.

NIS2 and DORA: Europe’s New Frameworks

NIS2, the successor to the 2016 Network and Information Security Directive, dramatically expands the scope of European cybersecurity obligations. It now covers over 160,000 entities across sectors such as energy, transport, health care, digital infrastructure and manufacturing, including medium‑sized enterprises【945144035004864†L171-L180】. NIS2 mandates incident reporting, strict vulnerability management, and supply‑chain risk assessments; companies must ensure that third‑party providers meet the same security standards. Boards and top management are held responsible for cyber hygiene and can face penalties for non‑compliance【960195212561326†L916-L945】. Complementing NIS2, the Digital Operational Resilience Act harmonises information and communication technology risk management for the EU financial sector【960195212561326†L947-L968】. DORA requires banks, insurers and fintech companies to conduct threat‑led penetration testing, manage third‑party ICT service providers, maintain business continuity plans and report major incidents to regulators【945144035004864†L120-L137】. It aligns operational resilience with cybersecurity, emphasising that failures in digital systems can pose systemic risks similar to capital shortfalls. Together, NIS2 and DORA signal Europe’s determination to create a common baseline of cyber resilience.

The Cyber Resilience Act and Supply Chain Security

The forthcoming EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) extends security obligations beyond critical infrastructures to manufacturers of “products with digital elements” such as Internet‑of‑Things devices, operating systems and software. Adopted in October 2024 with enforcement phases starting in 2025, the CRA requires cybersecurity by design, vulnerability management processes, and the provision of a software bill of materials (SBOM) for products so customers understand components and dependencies【960195212561326†L973-L999】. High‑risk products must undergo independent assessment before entering the market. Vendors must notify authorities of exploited vulnerabilities. These requirements respond to the growing reality that insecure consumer devices can serve as entry points for sophisticated attacks. By mandating SBOMs and secure development practices, the CRA aims to improve supply chain transparency and reduce the prevalence of vulnerabilities in connected devices. Coupled with NIS2’s focus on supply chain risk management, the Act underscores that resilience depends on the security of every link in the digital ecosystem.

Reporting Obligations and Incident Disclosure

In parallel with European reforms, the United States is tightening incident reporting. The Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA) mandates that designated critical infrastructure entities report substantial cyber incidents to the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency within 72 hours and ransomware payments within 24 hours【960195212561326†L1026-L1047】. The law will also require the publication of anonymised incident information to improve situational awareness. Enforcement rules are expected by March 2025. In the corporate sphere, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission’s new cybersecurity disclosure rules require publicly listed companies to disclose material cyber incidents within four business days and to provide annual reporting on risk management and governance【945144035004864†L251-L259】. These obligations reflect an increased regulatory focus on transparency and the expectation that boards actively oversee cyber risk. Through timely disclosure, regulators hope to improve collective understanding of threats and promote accountability. For organisations, they underscore the need to develop robust incident detection, response and reporting processes.

Achieving Compliance and Building Resilience

Meeting these diverse obligations requires more than a checklist; it demands a culture of security and resilience. Organisations should adopt risk‑based frameworks to prioritise resources and focus on critical assets and vulnerabilities. DORA and NIS2 emphasise continuous monitoring, threat‑led testing and third‑party oversight【960195212561326†L947-L968】【945144035004864†L171-L180】. The CRA requires security by design and supply chain transparency【960195212561326†L973-L999】. CIRCIA and SEC rules necessitate timely detection and reporting【960195212561326†L1026-L1047】【945144035004864†L251-L259】. To comply, companies must integrate cybersecurity into governance, appoint dedicated leaders with board‑level authority, and invest in training and awareness across their workforce. Regular exercises and external audits can reveal gaps and improve readiness. In the longer term, harmonising overlapping frameworks and adopting international standards can reduce complexity. Ultimately, regulation is not just a burden; it can be a catalyst for building resilient systems that underpin public trust and economic prosperity.
