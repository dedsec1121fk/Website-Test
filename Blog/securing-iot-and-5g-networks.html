Securing IoT and 5G Networks: Expanding Attack Surfaces and Defenses

With billions of connected devices, the Internet of Things and 5G networks create vast attack surfaces. This article analyses the evolving threats, regulatory responses and best practices for securing IoT in 2025.

Cybersecurity 10 min read 2026-01-26

Expanding Attack Surfaces

Over the past decade the Internet of Things (IoT) has transformed from a buzzword into a sprawling ecosystem of smart speakers, industrial sensors and connected vehicles. According to IoT Analytics, the number of connected devices reached 18.5 billion in 2024 and is expected to grow 14 percent to 21.1 billion by the end of 2025, with forecasts projecting around 39 billion devices by 2030【393265399562045†L80-L94】. Each new sensor, camera or controller expands the digital attack surface. Early IoT devices shipped with default passwords and unencrypted connections; attackers quickly exploited these weaknesses to build botnets that launched distributed denial‑of‑service attacks or mined cryptocurrency. Today’s threats are even more complex. Compromised smart thermostats and baby monitors now serve as footholds for intruders to pivot into corporate networks, exfiltrating sensitive data or deploying ransomware【683066031622300†L45-L92】. The coming decade will see billions more devices connected via 5G and eventually 6G networks, increasing bandwidth and responsiveness but also magnifying exposure. Understanding how the attack surface expands is the first step toward building resilient defenses.

From Nuisance to Crisis: Threat Evolution

As more organisations depend on connected devices to run operations, attacks that once merely slowed websites now threaten physical safety and economic stability. The IoT Evolution security report notes that campaigns have progressed from simple denial‑of‑service to complex infiltration of industrial systems【683066031622300†L45-L92】. Attackers co‑opt cameras and digital signage to hide malware or pivot into point‑of‑sale systems. In a widely cited case, a compromised heating, ventilation and air conditioning controller provided a path for attackers to access a retailer’s payment system, highlighting the danger of weak segmentation and shared credentials【683066031622300†L94-L112】. Ransomware operators increasingly exploit misconfigured remote access portals and insecure 5G gateways to deploy malicious payloads. Meanwhile, the growth of industrial IoT in critical sectors like energy, healthcare and transportation raises fears of catastrophic sabotage if attackers shut down pumps, interrupt production or manipulate medical devices. In the face of this evolving threat landscape, traditional IT firewalls are no longer sufficient; defenders need visibility across everything that connects, from factory floors to smart homes.

Standards, Regulations and 5G Security

Recognising the risks posed by insecure devices, governments and standards bodies around the world are implementing regulations to enforce baseline security. New laws require unique device identities, secure boot processes, automatic software updates and encryption by default【683066031622300†L94-L112】. The U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency and European regulators have introduced certification schemes to vet connected products before they reach consumers. Telecom operators are also hardening fifth‑generation (5G) networks: network slicing enables secure isolation for critical services, while zero trust architectures require continuous authentication and authorisation for devices and users. Microsegmentation limits the blast radius if a sensor is compromised, preventing lateral movement to databases or controllers. As networks evolve toward 6G, which will integrate sensing and communications and support terabit‑per‑second speeds【841078256563705†L237-L261】【48998544102414†L399-L445】, security will need to become AI‑driven and adaptive. Emerging standards will likely mandate cryptographic agility to protect against future quantum attacks and require vendors to publish software bills of materials.

Best Practices and Future Outlook

To protect IoT ecosystems, organisations must adopt a multi‑layered defence strategy. Security by design means selecting devices that support strong authentication, encrypted traffic and automatic updates. Network segmentation and zero trust policies should isolate sensor networks from sensitive systems and require contextual access controls. Continuous monitoring using AI‑powered anomaly detection can identify unusual patterns of traffic or behaviour and trigger containment measures. Patch management and vulnerability disclosure programs help close gaps in firmware and software. Since many devices operate within supply chains, collaboration between manufacturers, service providers and regulators is essential to ensure secure procurement and lifecycle management. Looking ahead, as 5G and 6G enable ubiquitous connectivity and real‑time analytics, the community must balance innovation with resilience. Investing in cybersecurity education and raising consumer awareness will be as important as technical controls. Ultimately, securing IoT and next‑generation networks will determine whether the promise of ubiquitous computing is realised safely.
