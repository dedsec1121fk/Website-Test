1) Overview

Coordinated campaigns win by repetition, emotional triggers, and manufactured consensus. Your defense is friction: verify, slow down, diversify inputs.

This post is written for practical use: what to do first, what to avoid, and how to explain the topic to someone who isn’t living in security-twitter.

2) Key takeaways

If you only remember a few things, remember these:

  • Look for coordination signals: identical phrasing, synchronized posting, and cross-platform amplification.
  • Separate ‘virality’ from ‘credibility’: check primary sources and original timestamps.
  • Build habits: pause, check provenance, and avoid sharing ‘just in case’.

3) Practical checklist

Use this as a quick audit. Do the top items first.

  1. Check account history: creation date, pattern of posts, and sudden topic shifts.
  2. Search for the earliest version of a claim; compare headlines vs full context.
  3. Use reverse image/video search and keyframe checks for recycled media.
  4. Follow a ‘two-source’ rule for major claims—at least one should be primary.

4) Common mistakes to avoid

Most failures come from predictable patterns. Here are the common ones for this topic:

  • Arguing with bots in public threads (boosts reach).
  • Assuming all manipulation is ‘foreign’—domestic actors do it too.
  • Sharing screenshots instead of links (screenshots hide context).

5) If you’re a team

If you’re doing this inside a team, add two layers: governance and rehearsal.

Write down owners, thresholds, and escalation paths. Then run a small drill (tabletop or dry-run) so the checklist becomes muscle memory.

10) Appendix D: Settings that help — where people get misled (Disinformation / Playbook)

Small settings changes often beat big tool changes. Start here.

  • Enable login alerts and security notifications on your key accounts.
  • Use a password manager and create unique passwords + passkeys where possible.
  • Lock down recovery: add backup codes offline, remove old phone numbers.
  • Disable legacy protocols and risky integrations you don’t need.
  • Use separate browser profiles for high‑risk accounts (email, banking).
  • Keep a minimal ‘admin’ device for critical actions if you can.

Pick two settings today. Momentum beats perfection.

11) Appendix E: If you’re a team — how it fails in the real world (Disinformation / Playbook)

Teams fail at the seams: shared access, unclear ownership, and ‘temporary’ exceptions.

  • Assign owners: every system has a human responsible for access and recovery.
  • Replace shared accounts with role-based access and audit trails.
  • Time‑box privileged access and review it monthly.
  • Write a one‑page incident checklist: who does what in the first hour.
  • Do a monthly restore test and a quarterly tabletop drill.
  • Keep an inventory of critical vendors, renewals, and key contacts.

You don’t need a big program—just consistent ownership and routine checks.

12) Appendix F: Questions to ask vendors — signals that you’re improving (Disinformation / Playbook)

Vendor questions for Disinformation Playbook: Spotting Coordinated Campaigns. Use these when evaluating a service or a tool you rely on.

  • How do you protect against phishing and account takeover?
  • What is your incident disclosure process and timeline?
  • How do integrations get access and how can we revoke it?
  • What security defaults are enabled at signup?
  • What controls exist for privacy and data minimization?
  • Where are data stored and how is encryption handled?
  • What logs and alerts are available by default?
  • Do you support least-privilege roles and audit trails?

13) Appendix G: 5‑minute drill — how to explain it to a friend (Disinformation / Playbook)

5-minute drill for Disinformation Playbook: Spotting Coordinated Campaigns. Run it now, then repeat monthly. Small drills build confidence.

  • Write a 5-line incident note and share it with your team.
  • Check recent logins and revoke an old session.
  • Pick one account and simulate a lockout: can you recover within 10 minutes?
  • Run your backup restore test on one file.
  • Review permissions for one integration and reduce them.
  • Update one device and verify encryption is on.
