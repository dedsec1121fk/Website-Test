5G and 6G: The Next Frontier of Connectivity

A forward‑looking analysis of fifth‑ and sixth‑generation mobile networks—timelines, capabilities, challenges and the opportunities they bring for society.

Technology 10 min read 2026-01-26

From 5G to 6G: Timeline and Evolution

Fifth‑generation (5G) networks are still rolling out worldwide, promising faster speeds and lower latency than 4G. Yet researchers and industry are already planning for 6G. According to Digi International, commercial 6G deployment is expected around 2030, following specification development between 2025 and 2029 and lab testing in 2028【841078256563705†L206-L224】. The 3GPP Release 18, known as 5G Advanced, will arrive commercially in 2025 and lay the groundwork for 6G【841078256563705†L212-L224】. Ericsson notes that 6G will build on 5G to create a cyber‑physical world where the digital and physical converge【48998544102414†L384-L399】. This evolution underscores that 5G is not a destination but a stepping stone to far more capable networks.

Ultra‑High Performance and Use Cases

While 5G delivers peak speeds up to several gigabits per second, 6G aims for terabit‑per‑second throughput, microsecond latency and reliability of “seven nines” (99.99999 %). Digi International explains that 6G will use frequencies above 100 GHz and feature advanced antenna technologies to achieve these targets【841078256563705†L237-L261】. Such capabilities will enable immersive extended reality, massive digital twins, holographic telepresence and integrated sensing and communication【841078256563705†L293-L299】【48998544102414†L399-L445】. Ericsson envisions 6G supporting autonomous mobility, ubiquitous robotics and precise positioning, merging communication with sensing to perceive the environment【48998544102414†L399-L445】. These applications demand networks that can transmit huge volumes of data with negligible delay and adapt in real time.

Challenges and Sustainable Design

The leap from 5G to 6G is not just about more spectrum. Using frequencies above 100 GHz introduces significant engineering challenges: radio waves at these bands have limited range and can be blocked by obstacles or rain. Digi International warns that designing RF components, antennas and chips capable of handling such high frequencies will require new materials and manufacturing techniques【841078256563705†L264-L281】. Processing terabit‑scale data streams may necessitate optical or quantum computing hardware. Another challenge is energy consumption; as network density increases, power demands rise. Ericsson highlights that 6G must be “sustainably designed,” using AI to optimise resource allocation and integrating renewable energy sources【48998544102414†L420-L473】. Balancing performance, coverage and environmental impact will be central to 6G’s success.

Preparing for the Future

To harness the potential of 6G, stakeholders must begin preparations today. Policy makers should allocate spectrum and fund research into advanced materials and protocols. Industry consortia must develop open standards to ensure interoperability. Companies should experiment with 5G Advanced features like network slicing and edge computing to build the skills required for 6G. Educators must train the next generation of engineers in millimetre‑wave physics, security and ethics. Most importantly, society must consider how to deploy this technology responsibly so that it benefits everyone. By planning early and adopting a holistic approach, 5G and 6G can power transformative applications while preserving privacy, security and sustainability.
