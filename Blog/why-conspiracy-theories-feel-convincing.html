Why Conspiracy Theories Feel Convincing

A psychology-and-media guide to the patterns that make extraordinary claims stick.

Overview

Why Conspiracy Theories Feel Convincing is easier to act on when you treat it as a set of small, reversible choices. Instead of chasing “perfect” setups, aim for a workflow that still works on your worst day: low battery, rushed decisions, and noisy information. A dramatic clip ‘proves’ a claim, but the original full video shows a mundane explanation.

Key ideas

Three ideas to keep in mind:

  • Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence—not just many posts.
  • Patterns: secret cabals, unfalsifiable explanations, moving goalposts.
  • Algorithms reward emotion; anger and fear travel farther than nuance.

Do this today

A practical way to apply this today:

  1. Ask: what would change my mind? (falsifiability).
  2. Trace the claim back to a primary source, not a screenshot.
  3. Check incentives: who benefits from belief or outrage?

Pitfalls to avoid

Common pitfalls: (a) trusting a single screenshot or single setting, (b) adding tools you won’t maintain, and (c) skipping the ‘test’ step. If you can’t test it, you don’t really own it.

Reusable checklist

Quick checklist you can reuse:

  • Update software and firmware.
  • Turn on the strongest account protection available.
  • Reduce what you share (data, permissions, extensions).
  • Keep one backup path and one recovery path.
  • Write down the 3 steps you’ll do if something goes wrong.

Mini glossary

Mini glossary:

  • Falsifiable: A claim that could be proven wrong by evidence.
  • Primary source: Original document/data, not reposts.
  • Moving goalposts: Changing standards after evidence appears.
  • Gish gallop: Overwhelming with many weak claims.

Wrap‑up

If you take only one thing from this post, make it this: choose fewer moving parts, document the few you keep, and rehearse once. That’s how “security” turns into muscle memory.
