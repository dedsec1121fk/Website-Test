3) Realistic scenarios

Think in scenarios, not headlines. These are common, repeatable patterns.

  • A message that looks official asks you to confirm or ‘fix’ something urgently.
  • A login alert appears for a device/location you don’t recognize.
  • A payment detail change request arrives when you’re distracted.
  • A ‘helpful’ tool/plugin asks for broad permissions.
  • An account recovery attempt hits your email/SMS unexpectedly.

4) The checklist (do this first)

If you do nothing else, do these. They remove the easiest paths attackers rely on.

  • Turn on MFA everywhere (prefer passkeys/authenticator).
  • Update automatically (OS, browser, apps, router/firmware).
  • Revoke old sessions/devices and remove unused apps/extensions.
  • Back up important data and run one restore test.
  • Write down recovery steps and keep backup codes offline.

5) Tools & settings that help

Choose boring, proven tools. Consistency matters more than complexity.

  • Password manager + unique passwords.
  • Security notifications (logins, payments, admin changes).
  • Separate profiles/accounts for high‑risk activities.
  • Device encryption + screen lock + biometric where appropriate.
  • A ‘clean’ device or profile for critical accounts if you can.

6) Common mistakes to avoid

  • Keeping ‘temporary’ access forever.
  • Reusing passwords or sharing accounts.
  • Ignoring recovery security (email/phone is the real master key).
  • Assuming backups exist without testing restore.
  • Trusting urgency over verification.

Security is mostly removing sharp edges from daily habits.

7) If you’re a team

Teams get compromised through shared access and unclear ownership. Fix the seams.

  • Assign owners for critical systems and accounts.
  • Replace shared passwords with roles + audit trails.
  • Time‑box admin access and review monthly.
  • Write a one-page incident checklist (first hour).
  • Run a monthly restore test and quarterly tabletop drill.

9) Mini glossary

  • Blast radius: how far damage spreads after one compromise.
  • Least privilege: only the access needed, only for as long as needed.
  • Out‑of‑band verification: confirm via another channel (call, in-person).

12) Appendix F: Questions to ask vendors

Vendors can widen your attack surface. These questions keep risk visible.

  • What data do you collect, and what’s the default retention period?
  • Do you support MFA/passkeys and SSO? Is it included or paid?
  • How do you log and alert on suspicious activity?
  • What happens on breach—notification time, support, and exports?
  • Can we export/delete our data easily? Is deletion verified?
  • Do you have a public security page or third‑party audit reports?

Even informal answers are better than assumptions.

13) Appendix G: 5‑minute drill

Practice beats panic. Run this drill with yourself or your team.

  • Step 1: open your account’s security page—find active sessions/devices.
  • Step 2: revoke one session (or sign out everywhere) and note what breaks.
  • Step 3: locate recovery codes/backup options and verify they exist.
  • Step 4: find where logs/alerts are viewed and who receives them.
  • Step 5: write down the ‘first call’ contact (bank, provider, admin).

Do it once now, so you don’t learn it during a real incident.

14) Appendix H: Metrics & signals

You can’t improve what you can’t see. Track a few simple signals.

  • Number of accounts with MFA/passkeys enabled (target: 100%).
  • Patch cadence: how quickly critical updates get applied.
  • Backup health: last successful backup + last restore test date.
  • Access hygiene: number of privileged accounts and shared accounts.
  • Incident latency: time from alert → action (aim to shrink it).

Metrics aren’t bureaucracy—they’re early warning lights.
