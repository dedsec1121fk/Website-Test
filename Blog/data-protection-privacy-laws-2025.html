Data Protection & Privacy Laws in 2025: From DORA to Global Rights

An overview of the evolving privacy landscape in 2025, covering DORA, NIS2, Law 25, India’s DPDPA, U.S. state laws and emerging individual rights and obligations.

Law 10 min read 2026-01-26

A Global Patchwork of Rules

Data protection and privacy laws are evolving rapidly, and 2025 stands out as a high‑stakes year in which multiple jurisdictions implement sweeping reforms. In Europe, the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) enters into force on 17 January 2025, harmonizing digital operational resilience requirements across banks, insurers and other financial services. The law imposes obligations around information and communication technology risk management, third‑party oversight, threat‑led penetration testing and mandatory incident reporting【945144035004864†L120-L137】. At the same time, the EU’s Network and Information Security Directive 2 (NIS2) expands cybersecurity obligations to an estimated 160,000 organisations across energy, transport, health care, digital infrastructure and other critical sectors, requiring stricter cyber hygiene, supply chain risk management and accountability of top management【945144035004864†L171-L180】. Beyond the EU, privacy reforms are proliferating. Quebec’s Law 25 introduces new individual rights such as data portability, mandatory privacy impact assessments for sensitive data processing and strict consent requirements for profiling【945144035004864†L191-L199】. India’s Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA), effective July 2025, establishes a modern privacy regime that emphasises notice, consent, limited retention, fiduciary responsibilities and heavy penalties for non‑compliance【945144035004864†L209-L217】. In the United States, five additional state laws for Montana, Iowa, Delaware, Indiana and Tennessee take effect in 2025, giving residents rights to access, delete, correct and opt out of personal data processing, including profiling and targeted advertising【945144035004864†L231-L244】. Finally, the EU AI Act enters its first enforcement phase mid‑2025, introducing a risk‑based framework and prohibiting unacceptable uses such as social scoring and real‑time biometric surveillance【945144035004864†L149-L157】.

Emerging Rights and Obligations

These new laws collectively broaden individuals’ rights and strengthen organisations’ responsibilities. Privacy regimes no longer revolve solely around notice and choice; they enshrine substantive rights to access, correct and erase personal data, as well as the ability to opt out of profiling. For example, the U.S. state laws coming into force in 2025 grant residents rights to access, delete and correct their data and to opt out of targeted advertising【945144035004864†L231-L244】. Quebec’s Law 25 requires businesses to conduct privacy impact assessments, obtain explicit consent before using sensitive information, and provide data portability on request【945144035004864†L191-L199】. The DPDPA designates data fiduciaries responsible for safeguarding personal data, limiting retention to what is necessary and ensuring swift breach notification【945144035004864†L209-L217】. DORA emphasises stringent ICT risk management and mandates threat‑led penetration testing and incident reporting within the financial sector【945144035004864†L120-L137】. NIS2 likewise requires essential and important entities to report incidents and to manage supply chain risk【945144035004864†L171-L180】. New requirements are also emerging in securities regulation: from 2025, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission will require public companies to disclose material cybersecurity incidents within four business days and to report annually on cyber risk oversight【945144035004864†L251-L259】. Even children’s privacy is under scrutiny as potential revisions to the U.S. Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) would extend protections to teens and limit AI profiling【945144035004864†L284-L290】.

Challenges for Enterprises and Compliance

While these reforms aim to empower individuals, they create a complex compliance landscape for organisations. Multinational companies must map and reconcile overlapping obligations across jurisdictions. A financial institution operating in Europe must comply with DORA’s technical resilience requirements, NIS2’s supply chain obligations and the EU AI Act’s risk assessments all at once. Meanwhile, its Canadian subsidiary must adhere to Law 25’s strict consent requirements and data‑portability rights, while its Indian affiliate implements fiduciary responsibilities under the DPDPA. Achieving compliance requires privacy‑by‑design and cross‑functional coordination among legal, IT and security teams. DORA mandates robust risk management frameworks and threat‑led penetration tests【945144035004864†L120-L137】; NIS2 demands executive accountability and incident response plans【945144035004864†L171-L180】. ISO/IEC 42001, the first international standard for AI management systems adopted in 2025, provides a baseline for documenting and monitoring AI life cycles【945144035004864†L267-L274】. However, aligning AI governance with data‑protection requirements and cybersecurity regimes requires significant investment in data inventories, risk assessments and continuous monitoring. Without clear global harmonisation, organisations risk fines, reputational damage and barriers to innovation.

Towards a Harmonized Future

As digital trade becomes borderless, policymakers recognise the need for harmonisation. The EU AI Act’s risk‑based approach is influencing debates in the United States, Canada and Asia, and some jurisdictions may adopt similar frameworks to ease cross‑border compliance【945144035004864†L149-L157】. India’s DPDPA underscores the importance of accountability and limited retention【945144035004864†L209-L217】; U.S. state laws embed opt‑out rights【945144035004864†L231-L244】. Civil society organisations call for privacy regulations that incorporate fairness, transparency and human rights, aligning with AI ethics. Advocates also urge governments to support interoperability through mutual recognition and cross‑border data‑transfer mechanisms that respect privacy. In the long term, a global data‑protection framework may emerge, combining regional best practices and principles of proportionality, accountability and procedural fairness. For enterprises, the path forward involves adopting a holistic data‑governance strategy that integrates privacy, security and ethical AI into the core of business operations. By anticipating regulatory convergence and centring digital rights, organisations can drive innovation while protecting individuals’ autonomy and dignity.
