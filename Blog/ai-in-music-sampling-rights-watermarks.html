4) The checklist (do this first)

  • Write down your “crown jewels” (accounts, data, devices).
  • Turn on MFA everywhere; prefer passkeys or authenticator apps.
  • Separate work and personal accounts; avoid sharing logins.
  • Update automatically and remove software you don’t use.
  • Back up with a restore test (a backup you can’t restore is a myth).

This checklist won’t solve everything, but it removes the “easy wins” attackers rely on.

6) Common mistakes to avoid

  • Treating “temporary” access as permanent.
  • Keeping data forever and widening the blast radius.
  • Reusing passwords or sharing accounts between people.
  • Skipping backups and relying on luck.

If you fix only one thing: reduce permissions and reduce data retention. Less surface area, less blast radius.

10) Appendix D: Settings that help

Small settings changes often beat big tool changes. Start here.

  • Enable login alerts and security notifications on your key accounts.
  • Use a password manager and create unique passwords + passkeys where possible.
  • Lock down recovery: add backup codes offline, remove old phone numbers.
  • Disable legacy protocols and risky integrations you don’t need.
  • Use separate browser profiles for high‑risk accounts (email, banking).
  • Keep a minimal ‘admin’ device for critical actions if you can.

Pick two settings today. Momentum beats perfection.

11) Appendix E: If you’re a team

Teams fail at the seams: shared access, unclear ownership, and ‘temporary’ exceptions.

  • Assign owners: every system has a human responsible for access and recovery.
  • Replace shared accounts with role-based access and audit trails.
  • Time‑box privileged access and review it monthly.
  • Write a one‑page incident checklist: who does what in the first hour.
  • Do a monthly restore test and a quarterly tabletop drill.
  • Keep an inventory of critical vendors, renewals, and key contacts.

You don’t need a big program—just consistent ownership and routine checks.

12) Appendix F: Questions to ask vendors

Vendors can widen your attack surface. These questions keep risk visible.

  • What data do you collect, and what’s the default retention period?
  • Do you support MFA/passkeys and SSO? Is it included or paid?
  • How do you log and alert on suspicious activity?
  • What happens on breach—notification time, support, and exports?
  • Can we export/delete our data easily? Is deletion verified?
  • Do you have a public security page or third‑party audit reports?

Even informal answers are better than assumptions.

13) Appendix G: 5‑minute drill

Practice beats panic. Run this drill with yourself or your team.

  • Step 1: open your account’s security page—find active sessions/devices.
  • Step 2: revoke one session (or sign out everywhere) and note what breaks.
  • Step 3: locate recovery codes/backup options and verify they exist.
  • Step 4: find where logs/alerts are viewed and who receives them.
  • Step 5: write down the ‘first call’ contact (bank, provider, admin).

Do it once now, so you don’t learn it during a real incident.

14) Appendix H: Metrics & signals

You can’t improve what you can’t see. Track a few simple signals.

  • Number of accounts with MFA/passkeys enabled (target: 100%).
  • Patch cadence: how quickly critical updates get applied.
  • Backup health: last successful backup + last restore test date.
  • Access hygiene: number of privileged accounts and shared accounts.
  • Incident latency: time from alert → action (aim to shrink it).

Metrics aren’t bureaucracy—they’re early warning lights.
