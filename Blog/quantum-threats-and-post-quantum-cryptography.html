Quantum Threats and the Race to Post‑Quantum Cryptography

Quantum computing promises breakthroughs but threatens to break today’s encryption. This post examines how Shor’s algorithm jeopardises RSA and ECC, highlights NIST’s post‑quantum standards and offers guidance for a secure transition.

Cybersecurity 10 min read 2026-01-26

Rise of Quantum Computing

Quantum computing harnesses phenomena like superposition and entanglement to perform calculations that are intractable for classical computers. Researchers have built small quantum processors and shown they can simulate molecular interactions or solve optimisation problems. Security experts worry that within the next decade a cryptographically relevant quantum computer—one large enough to run Shor’s algorithm on real world keys—could emerge. The SecurityWeek report notes that a sufficiently powerful quantum computer would use Shor’s algorithm to factor large primes and compute discrete logarithms, thereby breaking widely used public‑key encryption schemes like RSA and elliptic curve cryptography【33709127582273†L133-L156】. Although researchers believe such a machine may not be ready until the 2030s, adversaries are already harvesting encrypted data in a “steal now, decrypt later” strategy. Governments and companies must prepare for the eventuality that quantum computing will render today’s cryptography obsolete and threaten the confidentiality of digital communications, financial transactions and national security.

Breaking Today’s Cryptography

The cryptographic primitives that secure websites, email, banking and digital signatures rely on the assumption that factoring large integers or solving discrete logarithms is computationally infeasible. Shor’s algorithm dramatically alters that landscape by providing a polynomial‑time method for these problems. If a quantum computer can factor a 2048‑bit RSA modulus or compute an elliptic curve logarithm, it can recover private keys and decrypt all past and future communications. The National Institute of Standards and Technology warns that researchers are racing to build such machines and that agencies should begin transitioning to quantum‑resistant algorithms now【551343346115299†L167-L174】. Even data encrypted today could be vulnerable to future decryption if adversaries archive it. The quantum threat extends beyond encryption: digital signatures that underpin software updates, blockchain transactions and identity documents would no longer be trustworthy. Organisations must thus inventory their cryptographic assets, identify where public‑key algorithms are used and plan for migration before quantum computers reach maturity.

Post‑Quantum Standards and NIST’s Work

To counter the quantum threat, the cryptographic community has spent years developing algorithms based on mathematical problems believed to be resistant to quantum attacks, such as lattice problems, hash‑based constructions and multivariate equations. In 2024 the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology released the first set of post‑quantum cryptography (PQC) standards, recommending algorithms like CRYSTALS‑Kyber for key establishment and CRYSTALS‑Dilithium for digital signatures after an eight‑year selection process【551343346115299†L186-L197】【551343346115299†L248-L253】. NIST urges organisations and vendors to begin implementing these schemes immediately, since migrating global infrastructure will take years. The standards highlight the need for cryptographic agility: systems should be designed to swap algorithms without extensive reengineering. Other countries and standards bodies are following suit, and the European Union’s Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) is evaluating candidates. While PQC algorithms have been analysed for security and performance, they will continue to be studied, and further rounds of standards may add additional options.

Transition Strategies and Looking Ahead

Preparing for the quantum era requires long‑term planning. Organisations should begin by conducting a crypto‑inventory to catalogue where cryptography is used and which protocols rely on vulnerable algorithms. They can then prioritise systems based on sensitivity and exposure and develop migration roadmaps. Hybrid schemes that combine classical and post‑quantum algorithms can provide interim protection and allow gradual deployment. Cryptographic agility is essential: systems should support multiple algorithms and have the flexibility to switch as standards evolve. Network operators and software vendors must also plan for larger key sizes and the performance impacts of PQC. Public sector agencies should coordinate with international partners to ensure interoperability and avoid fragmentation. While the quantum threat is profound, the collective response demonstrates that with foresight and cooperation, society can transition to algorithms that preserve confidentiality and integrity even in the face of quantum computing.
