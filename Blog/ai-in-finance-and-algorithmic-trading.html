AI in Finance and Algorithmic Trading: Efficiency vs Risk

Examining how artificial intelligence powers trading, credit decisions and fraud detection, the efficiencies it brings and the systemic risks and biases it introduces.

Artificial Intelligence 10 min read 2026-01-26

AI Across Financial Services

Banks and investment firms were among the earliest adopters of artificial intelligence. High‑frequency trading algorithms ingest live market data and execute orders in milliseconds, exploiting tiny price differentials. Lenders use machine learning to score credit applications and monitor loan portfolios. Customer service chatbots answer questions at any hour. The U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO) reports that AI improves efficiency, reduces costs and enhances customer experience across these areas, but also introduces new risks【685970412945617†L63-L90】. Rapid automated trading has boosted liquidity and reduced spreads in many markets, while AI‑driven underwriting expands access to credit by analysing alternative data such as utility payments and mobile usage.

In fraud detection, AI sifts through billions of transactions to flag suspicious patterns. Machine learning models learn the behaviour of legitimate customers so they can spot anomalous transactions indicative of account takeover or identity theft. Some banks run AI‑driven cyberdefence systems that triangulate real‑time alerts from network sensors, user activity logs and external threat intelligence. By automating routine tasks and surfacing only high‑risk cases to analysts, these systems reduce workload and speed response times. Yet bias remains a concern: an algorithm trained on biased historical lending decisions may perpetuate discrimination when granting credit.

Benefits and Efficiency

Algorithmic trading offers significant advantages over human traders. Computers can process vast data sets and execute orders without emotion, reducing human biases such as overconfidence or panic selling. Speed is a major advantage: algorithms react to market signals in microseconds, capturing opportunities invisible to slower participants. Reduced transaction costs and tighter spreads benefit all investors by improving market efficiency. For regular consumers, AI‑driven robo‑advisors create personalised portfolios tailored to risk tolerance and financial goals.

Beyond trading, AI improves compliance by automatically monitoring transactions for suspicious activity and flagging potential money laundering. It can also help financial institutions forecast cash flows, optimise capital allocation and detect operational inefficiencies. In customer service, natural language processing answers queries and completes routine tasks, freeing human agents to handle complex issues. These gains allow firms to offer cheaper, more accessible services to consumers.

Systemic Risks

The same speed and complexity that make algorithmic trading profitable also generate systemic vulnerabilities. Algorithms may behave unpredictably when markets deviate from historical patterns or encounter rare events. Poorly designed strategies can trigger flash crashes, erasing billions of dollars in minutes. Technology failures or latency mismatches cause cascading errors across interconnected markets. The GAO warns that faulty data, overfitting and cybersecurity breaches can magnify losses【643175255603245†L33-L64】. Regulation lags behind innovation, leaving gaps in oversight.

Bias is another risk. AI‑based credit scoring may deny loans to minority communities if the training data reflects historical discrimination. High-speed trading firms can use AI to detect and exploit other market participants’ strategies, raising concerns about fairness. Concentration of algorithmic trading power in a few firms could also amplify systemic shocks. Regulators are exploring rules that require transparency in model design and stress testing. The GAO notes that some agencies lack authority to examine vendors providing AI services to banks, complicating oversight【685970412945617†L103-L116】.

Managing Risk and The Future

To harness AI’s benefits while mitigating downsides, a combination of regulation, oversight and technical measures is needed. Regulators are drafting guidelines on model governance, documentation and testing. Firms should pursue diversity in training data, monitor models for drift and enforce human review of high-impact decisions. Auditable, explainable AI can help analysts understand why a model made a recommendation. Companies must also invest in robust cybersecurity to prevent adversaries from manipulating algorithms or exfiltrating data.

The future of AI in finance will likely involve more collaboration between humans and machines rather than wholesale replacement. Traders may supervise automated systems and intervene during extreme events. Credit officers will challenge algorithmic outputs and contextualise them with qualitative information. Policymakers should update laws to address the unique risks of AI while encouraging innovation. If guided wisely, AI could make finance more inclusive and resilient; if neglected, it could amplify inequalities and instability.
