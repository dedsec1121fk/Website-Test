Economic Impact of Immigration: What the Data Shows

A comprehensive analysis of immigration's economic effects based on research from OECD, IMF, and leading economists. Examining GDP contributions, labor markets, taxes, and entrepreneurship with balanced, data-driven perspectives.

Society & Security 10 min read 2026-01-13

Introduction: Moving Beyond Rhetoric

Immigration remains one of the most debated topics in modern politics, yet discussions often rely more on emotion than evidence. While some claim immigrants are economic burdens who take jobs and drain public resources, others argue they are essential contributors to economic growth and innovation. The truth, as with most complex issues, lies in the nuances of the data.

This article examines the economic impact of immigration through the lens of peer-reviewed research, international organization reports, and empirical data. We will explore both the benefits and costs, acknowledging that effects vary significantly based on factors such as immigrant skill levels, host country policies, and economic conditions. Our goal is not to advocate for any particular policy position, but to present what rigorous research actually tells us.

GDP and Economic Growth

The relationship between immigration and GDP growth has been extensively studied by major economic institutions. According to the International Monetary Fund's 2020 report on advanced economies, a 1 percentage point increase in the migrant share of the adult population can increase GDP per capita by up to 2% in the long run. The OECD has found similar results, noting that immigration contributed approximately 0.3 percentage points annually to GDP growth in OECD countries between 2000 and 2010.

However, it's important to distinguish between total GDP growth and GDP per capita growth. Immigration almost always increases total GDP simply because there are more workers and consumers in the economy. The more relevant question for existing residents is whether GDP per capita—the average economic output per person—increases as well. Research from the National Bureau of Economic Research suggests that while high-skilled immigration consistently boosts GDP per capita, the effects of low-skilled immigration are more mixed and depend heavily on the specific economic context.

A 2016 report by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine in the United States calculated that immigration added approximately $54 billion to the U.S. economy annually. The report noted that first-generation immigrants initially cost governments more than they contribute, but this reverses by the second generation, who become among the strongest fiscal contributors. In the European context, a German Institute for Economic Research study found that the 2015 refugee influx would likely result in a net positive fiscal impact by 2025 as refugees integrated into the labor market.

Labor Market Effects: Jobs and Wages

Perhaps no economic question generates more debate than whether immigrants 'take jobs' from native workers. The economic research on this question is extensive, and the consensus is more nuanced than either side of the political debate typically acknowledges. The 'lump of labor fallacy'—the idea that there is a fixed number of jobs in an economy—is rejected by virtually all economists. When immigrants enter the workforce, they don't just fill existing positions; they also create jobs by consuming goods and services, starting businesses, and enabling native workers to specialize in different tasks.

A landmark study by economist Giovanni Peri analyzed the effects of immigration on U.S. wages from 1990 to 2006 and found that immigration increased average wages for American workers by 0.6% overall. The key mechanism was 'task specialization'—immigrants and natives tend to have different skill sets and therefore complement rather than directly compete with each other. For example, when low-skilled immigrants take jobs in agriculture, construction, or domestic services, it frees native workers to move into supervisory, communication-intensive, or other roles that require native language fluency and local knowledge.

However, research also shows that the effects are not uniform across all workers. Harvard economist George Borjas has argued that immigration does create wage competition for workers with similar skill levels, particularly among high school dropouts in the United States. His research suggests that wages for native workers without a high school diploma declined by 7.4% due to immigration between 1980 and 2000. The disagreement between Peri and Borjas largely comes down to methodological differences—how they define labor market competition and over what time horizon they measure effects.

In Europe, studies of specific immigration events provide natural experiments. Research on the Mariel boatlift—when 125,000 Cuban refugees arrived in Miami in 1980—initially found minimal wage effects on native workers. However, more recent reanalysis has shown modest negative effects on the wages of workers with less than a high school education. Similarly, studies of the 2004 EU enlargement, when Eastern European workers gained access to Western European labor markets, found small short-term wage pressures in specific sectors but overall neutral to positive long-term effects.

Fiscal Impact: Taxes Paid vs. Services Used

The fiscal impact of immigration—whether immigrants pay more in taxes than they receive in public services—is crucial for evaluating the effect on government budgets and, ultimately, on native taxpayers. The OECD's comprehensive 2013 study of fiscal impacts across member countries found that, on average, immigrants contribute roughly as much in taxes as they receive in benefits. The net fiscal impact typically ranges from -1% to +1% of GDP, with significant variation by country and immigrant characteristics.

Several factors determine whether immigrants are net contributors or net recipients. Age at arrival is critical: immigrants who arrive during their prime working years (25-45) tend to be net fiscal contributors because they pay taxes throughout their careers while not having consumed expensive public education. Education level matters enormously—highly educated immigrants almost universally contribute more than they receive, while those with limited education may use more services, particularly if they have dependent children. The structure of the welfare state also plays a role; countries with generous social benefits tend to see less favorable fiscal outcomes from low-skilled immigration.

Time horizon matters significantly. First-generation immigrants, particularly refugees and those with limited education, often have a negative short-term fiscal impact due to integration costs, language training, and credential recognition challenges. However, the National Academies study found that the second generation of immigrants in the United States has the most positive fiscal impact of any group, including children of native-born parents. This is because children of immigrants tend to achieve higher educational attainment and earnings than their parents, translating into higher tax contributions.

In Greece, the fiscal impact of immigration has been mixed. Studies by the Foundation for Economic and Industrial Research (IOBE) have found that documented migrants contribute positively to the social security system, helping to address demographic challenges in an aging population. However, the large influx of refugees since 2015 has created short-term fiscal pressures, particularly at the local level, though EU funding has partially offset these costs.

Entrepreneurship and Innovation

One of the most consistently positive findings in immigration economics is the outsized role immigrants play in entrepreneurship and innovation. In the United States, immigrants are 80% more likely to found a company than native-born citizens, according to the National Bureau of Economic Research. A 2019 study found that immigrants founded or co-founded 55% of America's billion-dollar startup companies, including Google, Tesla, eBay, and Yahoo. In the Fortune 500, 45% of companies were founded by immigrants or their children.

The innovation impact extends beyond startups. Research by economists William Kerr and William Lincoln found that increases in H-1B visa allotments (for skilled workers) led to significant increases in patenting by firms that employ these workers. Immigrants are disproportionately represented among Nobel Prize winners, Fields Medal recipients, and members of the National Academy of Sciences. In science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) fields, immigrants constitute about 30% of PhDs in the United States and are overrepresented among researchers at leading universities and technology companies.

This entrepreneurial tendency is not limited to high-skilled immigration. Research shows that immigrants of all skill levels are more likely than natives to start businesses. This may reflect selection effects—the same risk-taking personality that motivates migration may also motivate entrepreneurship—or it may be that immigrants face discrimination in the traditional job market and turn to self-employment as an alternative. Whatever the cause, immigrant-founded businesses create jobs for both immigrant and native workers.

Demographic Benefits: Addressing Aging Populations

Many developed countries face a demographic challenge: aging populations and declining birth rates are putting pressure on pension systems and healthcare costs. Immigration can partially address this challenge. Immigrants tend to be younger than the native population and have higher fertility rates, at least in the first generation. According to Eurostat, without net migration, the European Union's working-age population would have declined by 2.6 million between 2015 and 2020; instead, it grew by 1.3 million.

In Japan, which has historically resisted immigration, the government has begun liberalizing immigration policy due to severe labor shortages. Germany has actively recruited skilled workers from outside the EU to address gaps in healthcare, engineering, and IT. Even countries with high unemployment, like Greece and Spain, face sector-specific labor shortages in agriculture and hospitality that domestic workers are unwilling or unable to fill.

However, immigration is not a complete solution to demographic challenges. The UN Population Division has calculated that European countries would need extraordinarily high levels of immigration—far beyond what is politically feasible—to fully offset the effects of population aging. Moreover, immigrants themselves age and eventually require pension and healthcare benefits. The most sustainable approach combines immigration with policies that increase native birth rates, extend working lives, and improve productivity.

Costs and Challenges: An Honest Assessment

A balanced assessment must acknowledge that immigration does create costs and challenges. Initial integration costs—including language training, credential recognition, and social services—can be substantial, particularly for refugees and asylum seekers who may arrive with trauma and disrupted education. Research shows these initial investments typically pay off over time, but they require upfront public expenditure.

Housing markets can be affected in areas with rapid immigration. Research from the UK and Germany shows that large immigration inflows can increase housing prices and rents in destination cities, creating affordability challenges for both immigrants and low-income natives. This effect is most pronounced in cities with inelastic housing supply—where new construction cannot keep pace with demand.

Public services such as schools and hospitals can face pressure in areas with rapid population growth. While immigrants pay taxes that fund these services, there can be a timing mismatch—increased demand arrives immediately, while tax revenues and infrastructure capacity take time to catch up. Local communities often bear the costs of integration while benefits accrue at the national level, creating political tensions.

Conclusion: Context Matters

The economic evidence on immigration is more positive than negative, but effects are not uniform. High-skilled immigration is almost universally beneficial, while low-skilled immigration has more mixed effects that depend heavily on policy design and labor market conditions. Integration support, flexible labor markets, and smart welfare design can maximize benefits while minimizing costs.

Perhaps most importantly, the economic debate should not be the only consideration. Immigration involves human beings—people fleeing persecution, seeking better lives for their families, or contributing their talents to new societies. Economic analysis can inform policy, but it cannot answer the moral and political questions that democratic societies must grapple with. What the data can do is replace fear-driven rhetoric with evidence-based discussion, allowing us to have more honest and productive conversations about one of the defining issues of our time.
